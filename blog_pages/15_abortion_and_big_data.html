Abortion in the United States

With the overturn of Roe v. Wade in June of 2022 and the rise of anti-abortion legislation in the following months, women in the United States have lost significant protections to their reproductive autonomy and overall health. Roe v. Wade was initially decided in 1973, a decade before the Internet was invented. The Internet has signficiantly changed the way we live our lives and poses previously unimaginable risks to women's reproductive autonomy throughout the United States due to the types of data being collected about them and the ability of legislators to access this data.

Period Tracking Apps and Sensitive Health Data

Period tracking apps have gained popularity since their appearance in smartphone app stores by the early 2010s (I have personally used these apps for over 15 years and witnessed their evolution first-hand). Some of the main period tracking apps include Clue, Flo, and Eve.

Types of Data Tracked

Some of the main concerns about period tracking apps are the types of data that they collect. The main purpose of these apps is to track menstrual cycles, but they also allow users to track highly detailed data about sexual activity, mood, pain levels, and other health information.

Voluntary Disclosure of Data

A major concern about users' perception of menstrual tracking apps—and health tracking apps in general—is that users are unaware that they are voluntarily disclosing their health data to the apps and that this data is not protected by privacy legislation for health data such as HIPPA. This is a major concern because the data collected by these apps is highly sensitive and is being actively bought and sold by third party data brokers.

In the aftermath of Dobbs v. Jackson, Flo's Data Protection Officer, Susanne Schumacher, claimed:

Flo will never share or sell user data, and only collects data when we have a legal basis to do so and when our users have given their informed consent. Any data we do collect is fully encrypted, and this will never change.

However, Flo has been caught selling user data to third party data marketing and analytics firms, including Facebook and Google. The FTC finalized a settlement in 2021 for Flo's deceptive practices that misled users about the disclosure of their health data, which included sharing sensitive health data with third parties without users' knowledge or consent. This is especially concerning since Flo has over 100 million users. This use of dark design patterns strongly violates the rights of data subjects since they were not informed about the use of their data, did not consent to their data being used in this way, and were not given the option to opt out of this data collection. Of a similar concern, the Wall Street Journal first identified Flo's practice of selling user data in 2019. The most significant concern of this data collection in a post-Roe world is the ability of law enforcement to use this data to criminalize abortion. Data brokers are often indiscrminate in who they sell data to and often sell directly to the United States government. This means that the data collected by Flo could be used by prosecutors as evidence of criminal abortions. This is especially concerning since data aquired by data brokers is not always accurate and is often used to target vulnerable populations.

Location Data and Abortion

Similar concerns have been raised about the use of ridesharing apps and the legal pressure to disclose data as evidence of criminal abortions. In 2019, the New York Times published an article about the use of location data from apps such as Uber and Lyft to track the daily movements of over 12 million Americans. Concerns about the use of this data are not unfounded. Prior to 2016, Uber contained a developer feature called Uber God Mode that allowed employees to view any user's location data in real time. This feature was used to track the location of celebrities and journalists and was also used to track the location of a journalist who was critical of Uber's business practices. Uber settled a lawsuit with the FTC in 2016 for $20 million for the use of employees' "God View" mode.

Companies have already begun voluntarily selling data about people who have visited abortion clinics, and the collection of this data is further concerning since governments could require the disclosure of this data as evidence in criminal investigations.
